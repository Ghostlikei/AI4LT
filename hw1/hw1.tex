\section{Part B: Theory Problem}

\subsection{Problem 1}

\subsubsection{Choice of Grid}

Let
$$
m = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil
\quad \text{and} \quad
G = \left\{ \frac{k}{m} : k = 0,1,\dots,m \right\}.
$$
Then $G \subseteq [0,1]$ is a finite grid with
$$
|G| = m+1 = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1.
$$

\subsubsection{Existence of a Consistent Grid Threshold}

We claim that for every $\Delta$-separated threshold-realizable sequence, there exists some
$\widetilde{\theta} \in G$ such that
$$
h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t) = y_t
\quad \text{for all } t = 1,\dots,T.
$$

Since the spacing of the grid is $1/m \leq \Delta$, there exists a grid point
$\widetilde{\theta} \in G$ nearest to $\theta^*$ such that
$$
|\widetilde{\theta} - \theta^*| \leq \frac{1}{2m} \leq \frac{\Delta}{2}.
$$

Now fix any round $t$.

\textbf{Case 1: } If $y_t = +1$, then
$$
y_t = h_{\theta^*}(x_t) = \operatorname{sign}(x_t - \theta^*)
$$
and the $\Delta$-separation assumption implies
$$
x_t \geq \theta^* + \Delta.
$$
Hence
$$
x_t - \widetilde{\theta}
\geq (\theta^* + \Delta) - \widetilde{\theta}
\geq \Delta - |\widetilde{\theta} - \theta^*|
\geq \frac{\Delta}{2}
> 0.
$$
Therefore,
$$
h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t) = \operatorname{sign}(x_t - \widetilde{\theta}) = +1 = y_t.
$$

\textbf{Case 2: } If $y_t = -1$, then similarly the realizability and separation assumptions imply
$$
x_t \leq \theta^* - \Delta.
$$
Thus
$$
x_t - \widetilde{\theta}
\leq (\theta^* - \Delta) - \widetilde{\theta}
\leq -\Delta + |\widetilde{\theta} - \theta^*|
\leq -\frac{\Delta}{2}
< 0,
$$
so
$$
h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t) = \operatorname{sign}(x_t - \widetilde{\theta}) = -1 = y_t.
$$

Since this holds in both cases, we have shown that one hypothesis in
$$
H_G = \{ h_\theta : \theta \in G \}
$$
labels the entire sequence correctly.

\subsubsection{Mistake Bound Derivation}

Now run the Halving algorithm on the finite class $H_G$: on each round, predict by the majority
vote of the currently surviving hypotheses, and after a mistake eliminate all hypotheses that
predicted incorrectly. Because the sequence is realizable by $h_{\widetilde{\theta}} \in H_G$, the
Halving theorem implies that the total number of mistakes is at most
$$
\log_2 |H_G| = \log_2 |G|
= \log_2 \left( \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1 \right).
$$

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

So an explicit online learner is the Halving algorithm over the grid class $H_G$, with:

\begin{itemize}
\item choice of grid:
$$
G = \left\{ \frac{k}{m} : k = 0,1,\dots,m \right\},
\quad m = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil;
$$
\item size of the grid:
$$
|G| = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1;
$$
\item resulting mistake bound:
$$
M \leq \log_2 \left( \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1 \right)
= O(\log(1/\Delta)).
$$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem 2}

\subsubsection{Chosen Feature Map}

Take $d=2$ and define
$$
\phi(x) = (x,1) \in \mathbb{R}^2.
$$
For the true threshold $\theta^*$, define
$$
u^* = \frac{(1,-\theta^*)}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}}.
$$
Then $u^*$ is a unit vector, and for every $x \in [0,1]$,
$$
\langle u^*, \phi(x) \rangle
= \frac{x-\theta^*}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}}.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\operatorname{sign}(\langle u^*, \phi(x) \rangle)
= \operatorname{sign}(x-\theta^*)
= h_{\theta^*}(x).
$$
So this representation realizes the threshold function as a linear separator in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

\subsubsection{Margin Lower Bound}

Now consider any $\Delta$-separated threshold-realizable example $(x_t,y_t)$. Since
$$
y_t = \operatorname{sign}(x_t-\theta^*)
$$
and
$$
|x_t-\theta^*| \geq \Delta,
$$
we have
$$
y_t (x_t-\theta^*) = |x_t-\theta^*| \geq \Delta.
$$
Hence
$$
y_t \langle u^*, \phi(x_t) \rangle
= \frac{y_t (x_t-\theta^*)}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}}
= \frac{|x_t-\theta^*|}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}}
\geq \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}}.
$$
Since $\theta^* \in [0,1]$, we have
$$
1+(\theta^*)^2 \leq 2,
$$
so
$$
y_t \langle u^*, \phi(x_t) \rangle \geq \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$
Thus the sequence is linearly separable with margin
$$
\gamma \geq \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$
In particular, we may take
$$
c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$

\subsubsection{Norm Bound}

For every $x \in [0,1]$,
$$
\|\phi(x)\|
= \sqrt{x^2+1}
\leq \sqrt{2}.
$$
Therefore we may take
$$
R = \sqrt{2}.
$$

\subsubsection{Mistake Bound Derivation}

By the Perceptron theorem, the number of mistakes is at most
$$
\frac{R^2}{\gamma^2}.
$$
Using
$$
R = \sqrt{2}
\quad \text{and} \quad
\gamma \geq \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{2}},
$$
we obtain
$$
M \leq \frac{(\sqrt{2})^2}{(\Delta/\sqrt{2})^2}
= \frac{2}{\Delta^2/2}
= \frac{4}{\Delta^2}.
$$

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

One valid choice is:
\begin{itemize}
\item feature map:
$$
\phi(x) = (x,1) \in \mathbb{R}^2;
$$
\item unit separator:
$$
u^* = \frac{(1,-\theta^*)}{\sqrt{1+(\theta^*)^2}};
$$
\item margin lower bound:
$$
\gamma \geq \frac{\Delta}{\sqrt{2}};
$$
\item norm bound:
$$
\|\phi(x)\| \leq \sqrt{2};
$$
\item resulting Perceptron mistake bound:
$$
M \leq \frac{4}{\Delta^2}
= O(1/\Delta^2).
$$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problem 3}

\subsubsection{Why This Does Not Contradict Part 1}

There is no contradiction between the continuous-threshold impossibility phenomenon from lecture
and the result of Part 1 because the two statements concern different families of sequences.

The impossibility phenomenon from lecture applies to arbitrary threshold-realizable sequences. In
that setting, examples may appear arbitrarily close to the true threshold $\theta^*$, so the learner
may need finer and finer resolution to determine which threshold is correct. In particular, the
continuous class $\{h_\theta : \theta \in [0,1]\}$ cannot be treated as a fixed finite class in the
worst case.

Part 1 adds the extra assumption of $\Delta$-separation. This changes the problem substantially:
every example satisfies
$$
|x_t-\theta^*| \geq \Delta.
$$
Because of this gap around $\theta^*$, two thresholds that are sufficiently close to each other are
indistinguishable on every admissible example. Equivalently, once we know the threshold up to
resolution on the order of $\Delta$, we know all labels in the sequence. That is why a grid of size
$O(1/\Delta)$ is enough in Part 1, and the Halving algorithm can be run on that finite effective
class.

So the lecture impossibility result rules out a uniform finite-mistake guarantee for the unrestricted
continuous threshold class, whereas Part 1 proves a finite-mistake guarantee on the smaller class of
$\Delta$-separated realizable sequences.

\subsubsection{Comparison of the Two Bounds}

The two bounds are
$$
M_{\text{Halving}} = O(\log(1/\Delta))
\quad \text{and} \quad
M_{\text{Perceptron}} = O(1/\Delta^2).
$$
They scale differently because they come from two different kinds of arguments.

\textbf{Part 1: } The Halving argument is combinatorial. After imposing $\Delta$-separation, the
continuous threshold class can be replaced by a finite grid of size $|G| = O(1/\Delta)$. The
Halving theorem then gives a mistake bound logarithmic in the number of candidate hypotheses:
$$
M \leq \log_2 |G| = O(\log(1/\Delta)).
$$
This argument measures how many distinguishable threshold locations remain once the margin gap is
taken into account.

\textbf{Part 2: } The Perceptron argument is geometric. It does not count hypotheses. Instead, it
uses the margin bound
$$
\gamma = \Omega(\Delta)
$$
and the norm bound
$$
R = O(1).
$$
The Perceptron theorem then yields
$$
M \leq \frac{R^2}{\gamma^2} = O(1/\Delta^2).
$$
This argument measures how far the examples are from the separating hyperplane in the chosen
linear representation.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}

The logarithmic bound from Part 1 is sharper for this particular problem because it exploits the
special one-dimensional threshold structure very directly. Once the threshold is localized to one of
only $O(1/\Delta)$ relevant positions, the problem is essentially finite.

The Perceptron bound is more general but also looser here. It applies through a generic margin
analysis for linear separators, and that analysis depends on $1/\gamma^2$. Since the margin is only
of order $\Delta$, the resulting guarantee is of order $1/\Delta^2$.

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

Part 1 and the lecture impossibility result are compatible because Part 1 assumes an additional
structural condition, namely $\Delta$-separation. Under that condition, the threshold problem has a
finite effective resolution and admits a logarithmic Halving bound. The Perceptron analysis gives a
different guarantee because it measures geometric margin rather than the number of distinguishable
thresholds, which is why it scales as $O(1/\Delta^2)$ instead of $O(\log(1/\Delta))$.

\subsection{Problem 4}

\subsubsection{Problems in the AI Argument}

There are several mathematical problems with the quoted argument.

\textbf{Problem 1: } The class of continuous thresholds does \emph{not} literally have size
$O(1/\Delta)$. The hypothesis class
$$
\{h_\theta : \theta \in [0,1]\}
$$
is still uncountable. What is true is weaker: for each fixed $\Delta$, one can build a finite grid
$G \subseteq [0,1]$ with $|G| = O(1/\Delta)$ such that every $\Delta$-separated
threshold-realizable sequence is also realizable by some hypothesis in
$$
H_G = \{h_\theta : \theta \in G\}.
$$
So the correct statement is about the existence of a finite surrogate class on a restricted family
of sequences, not about the cardinality of the original continuous class.

\textbf{Problem 2: } Even if such a finite class $H_G$ exists, it does not follow that
\emph{every} online learner enjoys the Halving bound. The Halving theorem gives an
$O(\log |H_G|)$ mistake bound for the \emph{Halving algorithm run on $H_G$}. It says nothing about
arbitrary learners.

In fact, the claim ``any online learner'' is false. Consider the learner that always predicts $-1$.
Fix any $0 < \Delta \leq 1$, and consider the sequence
$$
x_t = 1, \qquad y_t = +1 \qquad \text{for } t=1,\dots,T.
$$
This sequence is $\Delta$-separated threshold-realizable by $\theta^* = 0$, because
$$
h_{\theta^*}(x_t) = \operatorname{sign}(1-0) = +1
\quad \text{and} \quad
|x_t-\theta^*| = 1 \geq \Delta.
$$
But the always-$-1$ learner makes $T$ mistakes. Hence no statement of the form ``every online
learner makes $O(\log(1/\Delta))$ mistakes'' can be true.

\textbf{Problem 3: } The phrase ``including Perceptron'' is therefore unsupported. Perceptron does
not inherit the Halving bound just because a finite consistent grid exists. Its guarantee comes from
the margin analysis of Problem 2, which yields a bound of order $O(1/\Delta^2)$.

\subsubsection{Corrected Statement}

A correct statement is the following:

\medskip

\emph{For every $\Delta > 0$, there exists a finite grid $G \subseteq [0,1]$ with
$|G| = O(1/\Delta)$ such that the Halving algorithm run on the finite class
$$
H_G = \{h_\theta : \theta \in G\}
$$
makes at most $O(\log(1/\Delta))$ mistakes on every $\Delta$-separated threshold-realizable
sequence. By contrast, the Perceptron guarantee obtained from the margin argument is
$O(1/\Delta^2)$.}

\subsubsection{Proof of the Corrected Statement}

Let
$$
m = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil
\quad \text{and} \quad
G = \left\{ \frac{k}{m} : k = 0,1,\dots,m \right\}.
$$
Then
$$
|G| = m+1 = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1 = O(1/\Delta).
$$

Now consider any $\Delta$-separated threshold-realizable sequence. By definition, there exists
$\theta^* \in [0,1]$ such that
$$
y_t = h_{\theta^*}(x_t)
\quad \text{and} \quad
|x_t-\theta^*| \geq \Delta
\qquad \text{for all } t.
$$
Choose a grid point $\widetilde{\theta} \in G$ nearest to $\theta^*$. Since the grid spacing is
$1/m \leq \Delta$, we have
$$
|\widetilde{\theta}-\theta^*| \leq \frac{1}{2m} \leq \frac{\Delta}{2}.
$$

We claim that $h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t)=y_t$ for every round $t$.

\textbf{Case 1: } If $y_t=+1$, then $x_t \geq \theta^*+\Delta$. Therefore
$$
x_t-\widetilde{\theta}
\geq (\theta^*+\Delta)-\widetilde{\theta}
\geq \Delta-|\widetilde{\theta}-\theta^*|
\geq \frac{\Delta}{2}
> 0.
$$
Hence
$$
h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t)=+1=y_t.
$$

\textbf{Case 2: } If $y_t=-1$, then $x_t \leq \theta^*-\Delta$. Therefore
$$
x_t-\widetilde{\theta}
\leq (\theta^*-\Delta)-\widetilde{\theta}
\leq -\Delta+|\widetilde{\theta}-\theta^*|
\leq -\frac{\Delta}{2}
< 0.
$$
Hence
$$
h_{\widetilde{\theta}}(x_t)=-1=y_t.
$$

So the entire sequence is realizable by one hypothesis in the finite class $H_G$. Applying the
Halving theorem to $H_G$, the number of mistakes made by the Halving algorithm is at most
$$
\log_2 |H_G|
= \log_2 \left( \left\lceil \frac{1}{\Delta} \right\rceil + 1 \right)
= O(\log(1/\Delta)).
$$

Finally, this does \emph{not} imply the same bound for Perceptron. The Perceptron analysis from
Problem 2 uses the representation $\phi(x)=(x,1)$, a norm bound $R \leq \sqrt{2}$, and a margin
lower bound $\gamma \geq \Delta/\sqrt{2}$. Therefore the Perceptron theorem gives
$$
M \leq \frac{R^2}{\gamma^2}
\leq \frac{2}{(\Delta/\sqrt{2})^2}
= \frac{4}{\Delta^2}
= O(1/\Delta^2).
$$
This is a different guarantee obtained by a different argument.

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

The AI argument fails because it confuses an infinite class with a finite surrogate class, and then
incorrectly transfers a Halving guarantee to arbitrary learners. The correct logarithmic mistake
bound is an existence statement for a specific learner, namely Halving on a carefully chosen grid.
Perceptron is governed instead by the margin analysis, which yields the weaker bound
$O(1/\Delta^2)$.

\section{Part C: Perceptron --- Implementation and Experiments}

\subsection{Question 1: Data Generation}

To generate data with a known margin and a known norm bound, I fixed the true separator to
$$
u = e_1 = (1,0,\dots,0) \in \mathbb{R}^d
$$
and then sampled each example in the form
$$
x_t = y_t \gamma u + \rho_t v_t,
$$
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $y_t \in \{-1,+1\}$ is sampled uniformly,
\item $v_t$ is a unit vector orthogonal to $u$,
\item $0 \leq \rho_t \leq \sqrt{R^2-\gamma^2}$.
\end{itemize}

This construction gives an exact signed margin:
$$
y_t \langle u, x_t \rangle
= y_t \langle u, y_t \gamma u + \rho_t v_t \rangle
= \gamma,
$$
because $\langle u, v_t \rangle = 0$ and $\|u\|=1$. It also enforces the norm bound
$$
\|x_t\|^2 = \gamma^2 + \rho_t^2 \leq R^2.
$$

In all experiments below I set
$$
R=1,
$$
so the theoretical mistake bound becomes
$$
\frac{R^2}{\gamma^2} = \frac{1}{\gamma^2}.
$$

The implementation runs the standard online Perceptron with update rule
$$
w_{t+1} =
\begin{cases}
w_t + y_t x_t, & \text{if } y_t \langle w_t, x_t \rangle \leq 0, \\
w_t, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$
I initialized $w_1=0$ and used fixed random seeds for reproducibility.

\subsection{Question 2: Scaling with \texorpdfstring{$1/\gamma^2$}{1 over gamma squared}}

For the main scaling experiment, I fixed $d=25$ and swept $\gamma$ over
$$
\{0.50, 0.35, 0.25, 0.18, 0.14, 0.11, 0.09, 0.07, 0.055\}.
$$
For each value of $\gamma$, I generated a sequence whose length was a constant multiple of
$1/\gamma^2$, ran $48$ independent trials, and recorded the number of Perceptron mistakes.

The empirical relationship is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mistakes-vs-inv-gamma2}. A log-log fit of
the average mistake count versus $1/\gamma^2$ gave slope
$$
0.998,
$$
which is essentially linear scaling in $1/\gamma^2$. So the experiments agree very closely with
the theorem's prediction about how the mistake count should grow as the margin shrinks.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/mistakes_vs_inv_gamma2.png}
  \caption{Average Perceptron mistakes versus $1/\gamma^2$, together with the theoretical bound $R^2/\gamma^2$.}
  \label{fig:mistakes-vs-inv-gamma2}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Question 3: Tightness of the Bound}

The bound has the correct order, but it is not tight in constant for these random sequences. Across
the same sweep, the average mistake count was between about
$$
0.344 \cdot \frac{R^2}{\gamma^2}
\quad \text{and} \quad
0.436 \cdot \frac{R^2}{\gamma^2},
$$
with mean ratio
$$
0.393.
$$
So in practice the Perceptron did noticeably better than the worst-case upper bound, even though the
growth rate still tracked $1/\gamma^2$ very closely.

Figure~\ref{fig:bound-ratio-vs-gamma} makes this more explicit: the ratio stays well below $1$
throughout the sweep, so the theorem is conservative on this data distribution.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/bound_ratio_vs_gamma.png}
  \caption{Average mistake count divided by the theoretical bound $R^2/\gamma^2$. The observed ratio stays well below $1$.}
  \label{fig:bound-ratio-vs-gamma}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Question 4: Dependence on the Dimension}

To test dimension dependence, I fixed
$$
\gamma = 0.08
$$
and varied
$$
d \in \{2,5,10,20,50,100,200\}.
$$
For each dimension I ran $36$ trials on a sequence long enough to allow the Perceptron to realize
most of its mistakes.

The result is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mistakes-vs-dimension}. I did \emph{not} observe any
explosive dependence on $d$. The mean mistake count rose from about $14.1$ at $d=2$ to roughly
$64$--$66$ for $d=50,100,200$, and the change from $d=20$ to $d=200$ was only about $3.3\%$.
So the data are not perfectly flat in $d$, but once the dimension is moderately large, the mistake
count essentially plateaus instead of growing with dimension. That is qualitatively consistent with
the theorem being dimension-free.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/mistakes_vs_dimension.png}
  \caption{Average mistakes as the ambient dimension changes at fixed margin $\gamma = 0.08$. The curve rises at very small dimensions and then largely flattens.}
  \label{fig:mistakes-vs-dimension}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Question 5: What Happens as \texorpdfstring{$\gamma \to 0$}{gamma -> 0}?}

For a smaller-margin sweep, I used
$$
\gamma \in \{0.20, 0.15, 0.12, 0.10, 0.08, 0.06, 0.05, 0.04, 0.03\}
$$
with $d=25$. The behavior is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mistakes-small-gamma}. As $\gamma$
decreased from $0.20$ to $0.03$, the average mistake count increased from about
$$
11.46
$$
to about
$$
388.96.
$$

This matches the lecture intuition from the threshold counterexample. When $\gamma$ is small, the
examples lie very close to the decision boundary, so the learner receives only weak geometric
separation. The Perceptron still has a finite mistake guarantee for any fixed positive $\gamma$, but
the guarantee deteriorates rapidly as $\gamma \to 0$. In that sense, the small-margin regime is the
finite-margin shadow of the no-margin threshold phenomenon from lecture.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{figures/mistakes_small_gamma.png}
  \caption{Average mistakes and the theoretical bound as $\gamma \to 0$. Both grow rapidly, reflecting the difficulty of learning from near-boundary examples.}
  \label{fig:mistakes-small-gamma}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Question 6: How I Verified the Implementation}

If AI helps write code for this part, I do not want to trust the first draft blindly. I built three
checks directly into the experiment script:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Single-update test: } starting from $w=0$, one misclassified positive example should
produce exactly the update $w \leftarrow x$.
\item \textbf{Generator invariant test: } every generated sequence is checked to ensure
$$
y_t \langle u, x_t \rangle \geq \gamma
$$
and
$$
\|x_t\| \leq R
$$
up to numerical tolerance.
\item \textbf{Tiny separable-dataset convergence test: } on a hand-written separable dataset,
repeated passes of the Perceptron must end with zero training mistakes.
\end{itemize}

These checks would catch subtle bugs such as dropping the factor $y_t$ in the update rule, flipping
the sign in the margin test, or writing a data generator that silently violates the claimed margin.
